Trip Back in Time, Hopefully I Am Not Too Late

A Review of The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo

I am pretty sure that I am about 5 years late in coming to appreciate The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo. The acclaimed thriller novel has produced a handful of critically acclaimed films and has become a piece of essential summer reading for thousands of thriller junkies across the world. I had meant to read the novel years ago, but just now set aside the time necessary to read/listen to Stieg Larsson's work. What I found as I entered the world of Mikael Blomkvist and Lisbeth Salander was at times horrific, but almost always well paced and mysterious so as to capture my complete attention.

My first thought on finishing TGWTDT was that I wished I could read in Swedish. I found the English version gripping, but in some ways highly contrived. I felt that some of the choices in expression were rather cliche and probably stand ins for the more elegant Swedish expressions that Larsson had used in his original version. However, the chunky and sometimes cliched language allowed the text to highlight the action, which did not require any subtlety. I found myself not worrying too much about the complexities of Blomkvist or Salander and their peers, instead appreciating the way that the narrative, at least in English, pushed the action. I never felt trapped in anyone's head. The moments of internal monologue were nicely situated inside of larger sequences, thankfully diminishing their importance given their rough nature in the English translation.

Beyond any intracacies of translation, the most interesting aspect of TGWTDT had to be the short hop into the past. Written and originally published in the first years of the 21st Century, TGWTDT contains marker after marker of our recent past. The emphasis on technology and the specfic descriptions of computers and other aparati help to establish a conflict in the setting. As a reader coming to TGWTDT about a decade after it was originally written, the world that the characters inhabit is both on the cutting edge of advancement and horrifically left behind. The technological tricks that end up saving the day feel horribly archaic, if only because they are so easy to consider now. In the post-Edward Snowden world, information security is not an entirely mysterious concept. Yet, TGWTDT's reliance on the specific description of technology gives the text an entirely futuristic atmosphere. As someone who was a young child during the time that TGWTDT is set, I found the contrast to be quite enjoyable. I found myself considering just what these now small advances in technology must have felt like for people like my parents; the anxiety of moving away from the analog world of someone like Henrik Vanger to the digital world of Lisbeth, a world that we currently expand more and more every day, is entirely present and in many ways entirely driving throughout TGWTDT.
